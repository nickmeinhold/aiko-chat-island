aiko · the radio room

A five-scene explainer

The radio room

You hold a key to the island’s front door — and never to its radio. That isn’t a gap in the design. It is the design.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

You never key the radio yourself

Voiceover

Picture arriving at the island. You walk up to a desk at the shore and speak to the harbourmaster: send this message, join that room. What you don’t do is stride into the radio room out back and key the transmitter yourself. You never touch it. You tell the harbourmaster, and the island puts your words on the air for you.

What that maps to: people talk to the gateway over ordinary web traffic (HTTP / WebSocket) — the desk at the shore. The island’s private message bus (MQTT) is the radio. The app never touches it. The gateway is the one voice speaking on the bus, on everyone’s behalf.
Scene 02

Two locks people mistake for one

Voiceover

Someone asks, sensibly enough, “shouldn’t we put a lock on the radio, so only the right people get on the air?” It sounds like one lock. It’s really two, quietly wearing the same coat. One lock decides which of the island’s own machines may key the radio at all. A completely different lock decides which people belong to the community. Different door, different key, different keyholder.

The split that matters: lock one is letting a service onto the bus — the island’s own gateway, room-keeper and directory presenting credentials to the broker. Lock two is letting a person into the community — handled up at the desk, over the web. Tangle them together and you can’t build either well.
Scene 03

The radio room has no door to the sea

Voiceover

Here’s why the radio’s lock isn’t urgent yet. Today the room has no door to the outside world at all — you cannot reach the transmitter from a boat, from the next island, from anywhere off this patch of land. The only ones who can key it are the island’s own three machines, standing right there inside. So the room being unlocked is genuinely safe: there’s no way in to walk through.

Grounded in the wiring: the broker exposes no outside port — it’s reachable only within the island. Its only clients are three co-located, trusted services. So allow_anonymous being on is safe while the room stays sealed. The note is blunt that this is a comfort of today, not a law.
Scene 04

The hole opens when two islands wire up

Voiceover

So when does the lock start to matter? Not when a person shows up — they were always going to the desk. It matters the day two islands run a cable between their radio rooms so they can talk. That link is the first time the transmitter faces open water. A neighbouring island — or something pretending to be one — could reach in. The real danger was never a stranger at your door; it’s an island on the other end of a wire.

Where the threat actually lives: it’s island-to-island, not person-to-bus — a federation link between gateways and brokers that doesn’t exist yet. Leaving the radio open is a latent hole a future neighbour could exploit. That’s why fitting the crew’s lock before the first cable is the honest, cheap move.
Scene 05

Fit the crew’s lock first

Voiceover

That leaves two jobs of very different size. The small, buildable one: give the island’s own three machines a proper key to the radio and turn the lock — done from the island’s own settings, ready before the first cable. The big one: making you a full voice on the radio in your own right, keying it yourself. That’s a deeper rebuild of how the whole thing is shaped, and it waits on work still coming from upstream.

Buildable now vs the north star: close allow_anonymous and have the services present credentials — small, and shipped from the repo the way the rest of the setup is. Making a person a first-class bus peer is the aspiration, aligned with aiko’s own native model, and it lands on its own timeline — not before.

The whole thing, in one breath

The key you hold is the door. The island keys the radio for you.

“Put auth on the bus” was really two wishes in one coat. Give the island’s own machines a key to their radio — a small lock, worth fitting before the first island ever wires up to a second. Make a person a voice on the air in their own right — the north star, on its own patient timeline.

And the deeper vision — auth living right at the point where each request is made, per owner, per room? It isn’t set aside. It’s where this road leads. Fit the near lock now, and you haven’t spent anything you’ll want back when the far one arrives.
