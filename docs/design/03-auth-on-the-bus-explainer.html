A six-scene explainer

The radio room

Follow a single message from your hands to a friend’s — carried across the island by its harbourmaster, who does the one job you never have to.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

You hand it to the harbourmaster

The idea

It begins with you, at the shore, with something to say — “hi Andy.” You step up to the desk and hand your message to the island’s harbourmaster. From here it’s theirs to carry: they know every berth on the island, they know which one is Andy’s, and they’ll see your words the rest of the way. You arrived holding a message; you leave having simply handed it across.

What that maps to: your app speaks to the gateway over ordinary web traffic (HTTP / WebSocket) — the desk at the shore. The gateway is the harbourmaster: the island’s one carrier, who takes your message and moves it onward. You bring it to the island’s edge; the island takes it from there.
The real machineryClient ↔ gateway over HTTP / WebSocket — the gateway is the sole participant on the internal bus.
Scene 02

Carried to Andy’s berth — not called across the water

The idea

So how does it reach him? The harbourmaster carries it inland, hands it to the island’s record-keeper to write down, and walks it out to Andy’s own berth on the far shore, where it’s put straight into his hands. It goes to him. What they never do is climb the mast and call your message out across the whole harbour on the open channel, for every passing boat to catch. It’s carried and delivered — addressed to a person, not thrown to the wind.

What that maps to: the gateway routes your message to the specific people it’s meant for, over their own connections — delivered and addressed, never posted to an open channel for anyone to overhear.
The real machineryDelivered to recipients over their own WS connections — the MQTT bus is internal transport between the island’s services, not a public broadcast.
Scene 03

Two locks people mistake for one

The idea

Now a fair question: “shouldn’t we put a lock on that radio, so only the right people get on the air?” It sounds like one lock. It’s really two, quietly wearing the same coat. One decides which of the island’s own machines may key the radio at all. A completely different one decides which people belong to the community — the question the harbourmaster settles right at the desk. Different door, different key, different keyholder.

The split that matters: lock one is letting a service onto the bus — the island’s own gateway, room-keeper and directory presenting credentials to the broker. Lock two is letting a person into the community — handled up at the desk, over the web. Tangle them together and you can’t build either well.
The real machineryTwo boundaries: service→broker auth (MQTT credentials) vs person→community auth at the gateway, over the web.
Scene 04

Today the radio only reaches its own crew

The idea

So about that lock on the radio. Today the transmitter reaches exactly three listeners: the island’s own record-keeper, its directory, and the harbourmaster — all standing together in one room. It answers to no one off the island, because there is nowhere off the island to call in from. So an unlocked radio is genuinely safe right now: the only hands that can reach it are already trusted, already inside.

Grounded in the wiring: the broker exposes no outside port — it’s reachable only within the island, and its only clients are three co-located, trusted services. So allow_anonymous being on is safe while the room stays sealed. A comfort of today, not a law.
The real machinerymosquitto binds no external port; only three co-located services connect — so allow_anonymous is safe only while the bus stays sealed.
Scene 05

The lock starts to matter when two islands wire up

The idea

So when does that lock earn its keep? The day two islands run a cable between their radio rooms so they can talk. That link is the first time the transmitter faces open water — and a neighbouring island, or something pretending to be one, could reach in. The danger was never a person at your door; they were always going to the desk. It’s an island on the other end of a wire.

Where the threat actually lives: it’s island-to-island, not person-to-bus — a federation link between gateways and brokers that doesn’t exist yet. Leaving the radio open would be a latent hole a future neighbour could exploit. That’s why fitting the crew’s lock before the first cable is the honest, cheap move.
The real machineryThe real exposure is a future gateway↔broker federation link — island-to-island, not person-to-bus.
Scene 06

Fit the crew’s lock first

The idea

That leaves two jobs of very different size. The small, buildable one: give the island’s own three machines a proper key to the radio and turn the lock — done from the island’s own settings, ready before the first cable. The big one: making you a full voice on the radio in your own right, keying it yourself. That’s a deeper rebuild of how the whole thing is shaped, and it waits on work still coming from upstream.

Buildable now vs the north star: close allow_anonymous and have the services present credentials — small, and shipped from the repo the way the rest of the setup is. Making a person a first-class bus peer is the aspiration, aligned with aiko’s own native model, and it lands on its own timeline — not before.
The real machineryBuildable now: allow_anonymous=false + a repo-committed password_file / acl_file. User-as-bus-peer is the upstream-gated north star.

The whole thing, in one breath

The key you hold is the door. The island keys the radio for you.

“Put auth on the bus” was really two wishes in one coat. Give the island’s own machines a key to their radio — a small lock, worth fitting before the first island ever wires up to a second. Make a person a voice on the air in their own right — the north star, on its own patient timeline.

And the deeper vision — auth living right at the point where each request is made, per owner, per room? It isn’t set aside. It’s where this road leads. Fit the near lock now, and you haven’t spent anything you’ll want back when the far one arrives.
