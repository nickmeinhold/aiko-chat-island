aiko · row me home

A five-scene explainer

Row me home

When your key is your identity, losing it should mean losing everything. Here is the lifeboat aiko builds in from day one — crewed by friends, not by a company.

Scene 01

Lose the key, and the island sinks

Voiceover

Here is the scariest question in a world where your identity is a key only you hold. What happens the day you lose it? There’s no company that kept a copy, no “forgot my password” link, no support desk to email. The old safety net was always someone else holding a spare. Take that away, and normally the island just… goes under. For good.

Why it’s real: whoever can put a fresh key on your account effectively becomes you. So “how do you get back in?” is the same dangerous power as “how does a stranger take you over?” — one door, two visitors. Design 05 is about who gets to open it.
Scene 02

On a calm day, you hand out spare oars

Voiceover

The trick is to build the lifeboat before the storm. On an ordinary day, while everything’s fine, you pick a handful of people you trust — your guardians. Each one holds a little seal that’s theirs alone. Not a copy of your key, not a piece of it. Just their own signature, ready to vouch for you if the day ever comes.

What the island keeps: only the public print of each guardian’s seal — enough to recognise their signature later, never enough to make it. Your account remembers n guardians and a number k. No master key sits anywhere. Nothing in a vault to steal.
Scene 03

Enough oars, and the boat moves

Voiceover

Now the day comes: your key is gone. You don’t beg a company — you go to your friends. You show them the new key you want to use, and you ask. Enough of them agreeing together can row you home. Not any one alone — a lifeboat needs several oars pulling at once, or it just spins in place.

How it locks: each guardian signs a fresh approval of this exact rescue — the specific new key you’re installing, not a blank cheque. The island counts them: k of n distinct guardians, or nothing happens. You chose k when you chose your friends. That number is your trust dial.
Scene 04

The rescue rings a bell first

Voiceover

But wait — what if it wasn’t you asking? What if someone leaned on your friends to row them in, pretending to be you? So the rescue never lands the instant the oars agree. It rings a bell and holds at the shoreline for a set time. Every device you still have lights up: “a rescue is underway.” If it wasn’t you, you shout — and it stops.

The safety catch: a valid quorum opens a waiting period, not an instant handover. Your existing devices are notified and can cancel before it finalizes — and a cancel from a device you still hold always beats the clock. An attacker can’t do this quietly, and can’t do it fast.
Scene 05

Why a living crew beats a buried key

Voiceover

You might ask: why all this — why not just lock one master key in a vault somewhere as a backup? Because a buried key is a sitting target. Steal it once, quietly, and you own the account forever, with no one the wiser. A living crew is the opposite: to move it, several real people must act, out in the open, with a delay — and any one of them can turn and warn you.

The heart of it: a stored secret can be taken in silence; a live quorum cannot. Recovery here always means asking real friends, so it can never happen without someone noticing. That’s the whole reason friends beat a vault — not convenience, but that silence is impossible.

The whole thing, in one breath

No one holds a spare key to your life. A few people you trust hold an oar.

Lose your key and you’re not stranded — you’re asking friends to row you home, out loud, with time for anyone to say “wait.” The rescue that can save you is, by design, one that can never be done to you in the dark.

The one thing this can’t remove — you are trusting your friends. A crew that all quietly agreed to betray you, all at once, could still row the wrong passenger in. That’s the honest cost of holding your own life in your own hands: choose your guardians the way you’d choose who gets a key to your home.
