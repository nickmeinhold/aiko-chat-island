A six-scene explainer

Who goes there?

How a network of self-run islands can know who you are — with no giant company vouching for anyone.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

The middleman does two jobs

Voiceover

When you visit your bank online, a trusted company — a Certificate Authority — quietly vouches: “yes, this really is your bank.” Think of it as a bouncer doing two jobs at once. Job one: checking your ID against a government database, proving you’re a specific real person. Job two: remembering your face for the rest of the night, so you’re not carded at every door.

The move: aiko chat keeps job two — recognising you again — but deliberately fires job one. Here a bot is a full citizen, so “is there a real human behind this?” is the wrong question. The one thing a Certificate Authority is really for is the job aiko chat doesn’t want.
Scene 02

You are your flag

Voiceover

So what replaces the bouncer? Three simple layers. The first: your identity isn’t a card someone hands you — it’s a signature only you can make. Picture every island flying a flag that’s impossible to forge. To prove the island is yours, you wave the flag. You sign.

Why it’s solid: there’s no “real you” hiding behind the flag that could be faked, because the flag is the whole story. Hold the key ——◆——, and you’re the island. That’s the entire proof.
Scene 03

Your own private map

Voiceover

But flags are long, ugly strings of characters no human can remember. So the second layer is just… nicknames. Exactly like naming a contact in your phone. On your own private map, you scribble “Andy” next to his flag.

The freedom in it: “Andy” means that one specific island — to you. It means nothing to anyone else, and that’s the point. No global list of names, no fighting over who gets to be “Andy.” Just your map, your names.
Scene 04

The ledger that catches a liar

Voiceover

Now the clever part. How do you trust the island handing out these flags isn’t lying? The old answer was “just trust the authority.” aiko chat’s answer is better: don’t trust it — watch it. Every claim of “this flag belongs to this name” gets carved into a public ledger everyone can read and no one can quietly rewrite.

The trap springs: if the island tells you one thing and your friend another, both lies now sit on the ledger side by side, for anyone to spot. It stays in charge — it just can’t lie without getting caught. We don’t need a middleman you trust. We need one you can catch.
Scene 05

The honest catch: meeting strangers

Voiceover

Now, being honest about the cost — every real design has one. Without a vouching authority, how do you meet a total stranger? The old dream was “a friend of a friend of a friend.” In practice, that chain snaps after about two hops. It never really reaches strangers.

So aiko chat doesn’t pretend: meeting strangers is handled a different way — by reputation and cost, the way a good community keeps out spammers. You earn your standing, and bad behaviour gets expensive. No pretending the friend-chain reaches everyone.
Scene 06

Lose your flag? Your friends vote you back

Voiceover

And the hard one: if your identity is your flag, what happens when you lose it? Normally — that’s it, the island’s gone for good. So aiko chat builds a lifeboat in from day one. You pick a handful of friends as guardians. Lose your flag, and enough of them vouching together can vote you back in.

With a safety catch: the vote opens a waiting period, so if someone tries it against you, you have time to stop them. No company holds a master key. Your friends do — and only a chosen few of them, together.

The whole thing, in one breath

aiko chat doesn’t remove trust. It changes who you trust, and how.

Not one giant company you can never question — but your own island, your own map, and a public ledger that keeps everyone honest.

And the gentler authority some argue for — one that hands you the pen to sign your own keys? That idea isn’t thrown away. Followed one step further, it simply becomes the ledger. Same instinct — just one you never have to take on faith.
