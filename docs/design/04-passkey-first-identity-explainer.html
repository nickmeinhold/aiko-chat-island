A five-scene explainer

The key only you can turn

How you sign in to your own island — with no company in the middle to ask, to phish, or to lock you out.

Scroll to explore
Scene 01

The old way: a stranger vouches for you

Voiceover

Signing in with “Continue with Google” is like getting into a club by having a giant bank phone ahead: “Yes, let them in — I know them.” Easy. But notice where your membership actually lives: not with you, and not with the club. It lives in the bank’s ledger. Fool the bank, and someone walks in as you. Annoy the bank, and it simply stops answering the phone.

Social sign-in means your account really lives on someone else’s servers — Apple, Google, GitHub. It can be phished, and it can be revoked. On an island built to answer to no one else, that middleman was always the odd graft.
Scene 02

The new way: a seal only your ring can press

Voiceover

Now picture something older than any bank: a signet ring. The device already in your pocket holds a secret shaped exactly like one. It can press a wax seal that nobody else can forge — and here’s the trick — it never lets the ring itself leave your hand. Not a password you type out and might whisper to the wrong person. A mark that only your ring can make.

A passkey is a secret key held by your own device. Its private half never leaves the device — so there is nothing to type, nothing to reuse across sites, and nothing you can be tricked into handing over. Hold the ring, and you are the island.
Scene 03

Signing in is just proving you hold it

Voiceover

So how do you actually get in? The island holds up a riddle only your ring can answer — and a brand-new one every single time. Press your seal on it, and the gate swings open. No company is phoned. No password sails across the water to be snatched mid-crossing. There is simply no one in the middle to ask, to bribe, or to fool.

The ceremony: the island sends a one-time challenge, your device signs it, the island checks that signature against your public half. Because the riddle is fresh each time, a stolen recording is worthless — and there is no third party in the loop to phish or coerce.
Scene 04

Passkey-first: show the middleman the door

Voiceover

Here’s the part that makes it “passkey-first.” Once your own device is the key, the old vouching-company has no job left to do. So aiko doesn’t keep it around “just in case” — it hauls the whole thing overboard. The tangle of asking Apple, asking Google, remembering who vouched for whom: gone. The front door of your island is simply your own hand.

Passkey-first makes the passkey the identity root and deletes the social sign-in machinery — not disabled, removed. It was the largest, riskiest, most outside-dependent piece of the old front door. Fewer moving parts, no outside dependency, nothing left to keep in sync. Subtraction is the win.
Scene 05

The honest cost: one key, so guard it

Voiceover

Every honest design names its price — so here’s ours. If your own device holds the only key, then losing that device matters. There’s no giant company to email for a reset, because there’s no giant company at all. That is the exact flip side of owing no one: nobody else is keeping a spare for you.

The trade is self-custody: the key is in your hands, and so is the care of it. But aiko doesn’t leave you stranded — the answer isn’t a company’s master key, it’s a handful of trusted friends who can vote you back in. That lifeboat is the next note. → design 05 · social recovery

The whole thing, in one breath

The front door of your island opens to your own hand — and asks no one’s permission.

No bank to phone, no membership kept in someone else’s ledger, no password to lose in the crossing. Just a mark only your ring can make, checked fresh every time you knock.

And the fear this raises — “but what if I lose it?” — isn’t waved away. It’s the one honest cost of owing no one, and it has its own answer: not a company’s master key, but a chosen few of your friends. The next note picks it up.
